Structure Comparative Adjectives

To form a comparative adjective, you can add "-er" to the end of a one-syllable adjective, or use the word "more" before a multi-syllable adjective:



One-syllable adjectives
Add "-er" to the end of the adjective. For example, "It's colder today than yesterday"


Multi-syllable adjectives
Use the word "more" before the adjective. For example, "It's getting harder and harder to make ends meet". You can also use "less" and "the least" to compare qualities that are smaller in amount relative to others
Here are some examples of comparative adjectives in a sentence:

  • My house is larger than hers.
  • This box is smaller than the one I lost.
  • Your dog runs faster than Jim's dog.
  • The rock flew higher than the roof.

The structure Of Superlative Adverbs Depends On The Type Of Adverb


One-syllable adverbs
Add "-est" to form the superlative. For example, "fast" becomes "fastest".
Adverbs ending in "-ly"
Add "most" or "least" to form the superlative. For example, "most carefully" and "least thoughtfully".
Irregular adverbs
Memorize the superlative form. For example, "well" becomes "best", "badly" becomes "worst", and "little" becomes "least".
Short adverbs that don't end in "-ly"
Add "-er" to form the comparative and "-est" to form the superlative. If the adverb ends in "e", remove it before adding the ending.

Superlative adverbs describe differences or variations among three or more people or things. They always appear after a verb in a sentence, and in most cases, the word "the" precedes the superlative adverb. For example, in the sentence "Jian talks the loudest of all the members", "loudest" is the superlative adverb.
Examples

  • One-syllable adverbs: "Fast" becomes "fastest".
  • Adverbs ending in "-ly": "Most carefully" and "least thoughtfully".
  • Irregular adverbs: "Well" becomes "best", "badly" becomes "worst", and "little" becomes "least".
